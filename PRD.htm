Product Requirements Document: Utility-Scale PV-BESS Revenue Optimizer

1. Executive Summary

Product Goal: To develop a high-fidelity, physics-based "Digital Twin" simulation platform that empowers utility-scale investors and asset managers to rigorously quantify the financial and operational value of integrating Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) with Photovoltaic (PV) assets. The tool serves as a critical decision-support engine, moving beyond back-of-the-envelope calculations to granular, hour-by-hour revenue modeling.

Core Value Proposition:

The renewable energy market is shifting from subsidized Feed-in-Tariffs to merchant-based revenue models. In this environment, simple metrics like "energy independence" or "self-consumption" are insufficient for investment committees. This tool introduces Net Present Value (NPV) Optimization as the primary north star. By simulating the interaction between solar generation profiles and volatile spot market prices, the tool demonstrates how batteries can:

1.    Leverage Price Arbitrage: Buy low (or free) and sell high during evening peaks.

2.    Mitigate Cannibalization: Store energy during midday solar peaks when market prices are depressed due to oversupply.

3.    Avoid Penalties: Absorb energy during negative pricing events, effectively getting paid to charge.

4.    Prevent Curtailment: Capture energy that would otherwise be clipped due to grid connection limits.

Target Audience:

●     Infrastructure Investors (Greenfield): Venture capital and institutional investors evaluating new hybrid projects who need to determine the optimal capital allocation between solar modules and storage capacity to maximize the Project IRR (Internal Rate of Return).

●     Asset Managers (Brownfield): Operators of existing solar parks facing eroding revenues due to price cannibalization. They need to calculate the "Incremental IRR" of retrofitting a BESS to an existing grid connection to revitalize the asset's financial performance.

2. User Journey (The "Compare & Convince" Flow)

The application guides the user through a comparative narrative, starting with the limitations of a standalone PV system and culminating in a validated business case for storage integration.

Stage 1: The PV Baseline (The Anchor)

●     User Input:

○     Scenario Selection: The user toggles between "New Project" (Greenfield) or "Existing Park" (Brownfield). This selection fundamentally alters the financial calculations (sunk costs vs. total CAPEX) in Stage 3.

○     Location Intelligence: The user drops a pin on an interactive map. The system strictly ties this location to the correct electricity bidding zone (e.g., classifying a pin in Munich as "DE-LU" for Germany/Luxembourg market data) ensuring price accuracy.

○     PV Configuration: The user inputs the defining characteristics: Capacity (MWp), Tracking Mode (Fixed Tilt vs. Single-Axis), and Construction Cost (/Wp).

●     System Action (The Simulation):

○     Data Ingestion: The backend creates a localized weather model by fetching TMY (Typical Meteorological Year) or historical solar radiation data from PVGIS. Simultaneously, it pulls hourly Day-Ahead Market (DAM) spot prices from the ENTSO-E Transparency Platform for the matched zone.

○     Generation Modeling: The engine runs a full 8,760-hour generation simulation, applying specific loss factors to determine the net energy exported to the grid.

●     Output (The Problem Statement):

○     Revenue Baseline: "Baseline Annual Revenue: 3.2M."

○     Cannibalization Analysis: The dashboard highlights the "Capture Price" vs. the "Baseload Price." It explicitly quantifies Cannibalization Lossshowing the user that while the average market price might be 80/MWh, their solar asset only realized 50/MWh because it produced 100% of its volume during the cheapest hours of the day.

Stage 2: The Battery Solution (The Upsell)

●     User Action: The user acknowledges the revenue gap and clicks "Add Storage Solution."

●     System Action (Auto-Sizing Heuristics):

○     Instead of asking the user to guess battery specs, the system proposes an optimized starting point based on industry ratios (e.g., BESS Power at ~20-40% of PV Capacity; Duration at 2-4 hours). This reduces friction and provides a sensible "Smart Default."

●     System Action (Algorithmic Optimization):

○     The backend initiates the Linear Programming (MILP) solver. It does not simply "fill the battery when the sun is out." It solves for the optimal dispatch schedule that maximizes profit while adhering to cycle life costs and physical constraints.

●     Output (The Value Bridge):

○     Waterfall Chart: A visual breakdown of where the extra money comes from:

■     Bar 1: Base PV Revenue.

■     Bar 2: Arbitrage Gain (Revenue from shifting midday energy to evening peaks).

■     Bar 3: Negative Price Savings (Cost avoidance by stopping exports or charging during negative pricing).

■     Bar 4: Curtailment Recapture (Revenue from energy that would have been clipped).

○     Curtailment Analysis: A specific chart showing "Wasted Energy." If the battery hits 100% SoC frequently while prices are negative or generation is high, this visual data drives the argument for upsizing the battery capacity.

Stage 3: The Investment Decision

●     User Action: The user performs sensitivity analysis by adjusting BESS parameters (e.g., increasing duration from 2h to 4h) or stress-testing financial assumptions (e.g., increasing Battery CAPEX to simulate supply chain spikes).

●     Output (Financial Rigor):

○     Greenfield: Displays Project IRR, calculating the return on the combined investment. It answers: "Is this hybrid plant a better investment than a standard bond or equity fund?"

○     Brownfield: Displays Incremental IRR, isolating the return specifically on the BESS CAPEX. It answers: "If I spend 10M on a battery today, how fast will the arbitrage revenue pay it back?"

3. Engineering Models (The "Digital Twin")

To gain the trust of utility-scale engineers, the simulation must be grounded in physics, not approximations.

A. PV Model (The Generator)

●     Input Data: Hourly Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI) and temperature data from PVGIS.

●     Physics Logic & Loss Modeling:

○     Tracking Dynamics: The model distinguishes between Fixed Tilt (standard bell curve generation) and Single-Axis Tracking (broader "shoulders" morning/evening). Tracking is critical for arbitrage as it produces more energy closer to peak price hours.

○     Complex Loss Factors:

■     IAM (Incidence Angle Modifier): Accounts for reflection losses when the sun is at a low angle (sunrise/sunset), often reducing theoretical output by 3-5% during these hours.

■     Soiling & Snow: Applies variable monthly loss coefficients (e.g., 2% in summer dust, 15% in winter snow) rather than a flat annual rate.

■     System Balance of Plant: A standard ~14% deduction covering DC/AC conversion efficiency, ohmic cable losses, and transformer losses.

○     Long-Term Degradation: Applies a compounding linear reduction (e.g., 0.5% / year) to the output. Year 15 generation will be significantly lower than Year 1, impacting the long-term cash flow model.

B. BESS Model (The Storage)

●     Chemistry Assumption: The model hardcodes LFP (Lithium Iron Phosphate) characteristics, as this is the dominant chemistry for stationary storage due to safety and cycle life profiles.

●     Physical Constraints (The Boundaries):

○     Power Limit: $P_{charge}(t) \le P_{max\_inverter}$. The system respects the C-rate limits.

○     Energy Constraints: $SoC_{min} \le SoC(t) \le SoC_{max}$. The model enforces a buffer (e.g., keeping SoC between 5% and 95%) to protect battery health, meaning "100 MWh" of nameplate capacity might only offer "90 MWh" of usable throughput.

○     Self-Discharge: Models the parasitic loss of energy over time (0.05% / hour), ensuring that storing energy for weeks is penalized appropriately.

●     Degradation Logic (Capacity Fade):

○     Formula: $Capacity_{n} = Capacity_{0} \times (1 - \text{FadeRate})^n$.

○     Implication: A battery installed today will have ~20-30% less capacity in 10 years. The revenue model must account for this shrinking asset base.

C. Optimization Algorithm (The "Brain")

This is the core differentiator. We do not use rule-based logic (e.g., "if price > 50, sell"). We use mathematical optimization to find the mathematically perfect dispatch.

●     Method: Mixed-Integer Linear Programming (MILP) utilizing open-source solvers like PuLP or Pyomo.

●     Objective Function:
The goal is to maximize the Net Profit over the simulation horizon:$$ \text{Maximize } Z = \sum_{t=0}^{8760} (\text{Revenue}(t) - \text{CycleCost}(t))$$

●     Operational Amortization (The "Cycle Cost"):

○     Batteries degrade with use. To prevent "micro-cycling" (trading for tiny profits that don't justify the wear and tear), we implement a virtual penalty.

○     Formula: $\text{CycleCost (/MWh)} = \frac{\text{BESS CAPEX}}{\text{Cycle Life} \times \text{Capacity}}$.

○     Logic: If the spread between buying price and selling price is less than the Cycle Cost + Efficiency Loss, the algorithm will choose to stay idle. This preserves the asset for high-value trades.

●     Curtailment Logic:

○     The solver tracks "Missed Opportunity." If $SoC = 100\%$ and grid prices are negative (requiring the PV plant to shut down), the potential energy that could have been generated is recorded as Curtailment. This metric drives the business case for larger storage.

5. Technology Stack

Frontend (User Interface)

●     Framework: React + Vite (TypeScript). This combination ensures a highly responsive, single-page application experience with type safety for complex financial data structures.

●     Styling: Tailwind CSS. Allows for rapid development of a clean, professional, "FinTech-style" dashboard UI.

●     Visuals:

○     Map Integration: react-map-gl (Mapbox) or leaflet for precise location pinning.

○     Charting Engine: recharts is utilized for high-performance rendering of time-series data (8760 points), enabling zoomable Heatmaps, Power Flow diagrams, and Waterfall financial charts.

●     State Management: React Context API. Given the localized scope of the simulation state, Redux is unnecessary overhead; Context provides sufficient global state handling for the project configuration.

Backend (Simulation Engine)

●     Framework: Python (FastAPI). Python is non-negotiable for scientific computing. FastAPI provides high-performance async endpoints to handle multiple concurrent simulations.

●     Solver: PuLP or Pyomo. These are Python-based interfaces for mathematical optimization. They allow the definition of constraints and objectives in code and interface with solvers like CBC or GLPK.

●     Data Processing: pandas. Essential for efficient handling, resampling, and merging of hourly time-series dataframes (Weather + Price + Generation).

●     API Structure:

○     /api/optimize: The heavy lifting endpoint. Receives the full Project JSON, runs the physics/financial simulation, and returns the Result JSON.

○     /api/market-data: An internal helper endpoint that proxies requests to ENTSO-E to fetch and cache pricing data.

External Data APIs

●     Pricing:

○     ENTSO-E Transparency Platform: The primary source for EU spot prices.

○     Grid Status: A wrapper API for US ISO data (CAISO, ERCOT, PJM).

○     AEMO NEMWeb: For Australian market data (via CSV fetch).

●     Solar: PVGIS (European Commission) API for location-specific solar radiation and temperature data.

6. Metrics & KPIs (The Dashboard)

The dashboard presents results in three distinct layers of depth.

1.    Financial Metrics (The Executive View):

○     NPV (Net Present Value): The sum of all future cash flows discounted to today's value. A positive NPV indicates a profitable project.

○     IRR (Internal Rate of Return): The annualized effective compounded return rate. This is the primary metric investors compare against their "Hurdle Rate."

○     Payback Period: The specific year in which cumulative profits exceed total CAPEX.

2.    Operational Metrics (The Engineer View):

○     Self-Consumption Rate: The percentage of PV energy used to charge the battery vs. exported directly.

○     Cycles per Year: Expressed as "Equivalent Full Cycles." This confirms if the battery is being utilized efficiently (e.g., 250 cycles/year) or overworked/underworked.

3.    Efficiency Metrics (The Optimization View):

○     Curtailment Rate: The percentage of potential PV energy that was lost due to grid limits or negative prices.

○     Revenue Uplift: The percentage increase in total revenue achieved by adding the battery compared to the PV-only baseline (e.g., "+22% Revenue").

7. Limitations & Disclaimers

●     Historical Simulation vs. Forecasting: The results are a "backtest" based on historical weather and price years (e.g., simulating performance as if the system existed in 2023). Future years are modeled by repeating this historical profile with degradation applied. It does not predict 2030 market price dynamics.

●     Perfect Foresight: The MILP optimizer assumes it knows the electricity price for the next 24-48 hours with 100% accuracy. In reality, traders rely on forecasts. Real-world revenue is typically 5-10% lower than the simulation due to forecast errors.

●     Ancillary Services Exclusion: This MVP version models Day-Ahead Energy Arbitrage only. It currently excludes revenue from Frequency Containment Reserves (FCR), aFRR, or Capacity Markets, which are complex to model but offers significant additional revenue upside.
